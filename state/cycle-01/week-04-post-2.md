Most knowledge management transformations fail before they start.

Not because of the technology. Because of who owns the technology.

I spent time with a mid-sized professional services firm that did something most organizations refuse to do: they made an explicit org design decision *before* they touched a single AI tool.

Here's what they got right that almost everyone gets wrong:

**They didn't give it to IT. They didn't give it to the Chief of Staff. They created a hybrid role** — part domain expert, part AI translator — sitting inside the practice groups themselves.

The result? 90 days later, their knowledge retrieval time dropped by 60%. Junior staff were producing first drafts that used to require senior review just to get started. And the senior people? They stopped being librarians and started being editors.

The org design decisions that made it work:

→ Ownership sat with practitioners, not technologists
→ They defined "good output" before they defined "good tool"
→ They built feedback loops into the workflow, not just the model
→ They accepted that the first version would be embarrassing

That last one matters more than people admit. Most firms won't ship something imperfect internally. So nothing ships at all.

This isn't a technology story. It's a power and accountability story dressed up as a technology story.

The firms winning at AI right now aren't the ones with the best tools. They're the ones who decided who's responsible when the output is wrong.
