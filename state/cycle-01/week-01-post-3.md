We shipped a beautiful AI pilot.

92% accuracy. Sub-second response times. The team was proud. Leadership was impressed. We had a demo that made people lean forward in their chairs.

Six months later, nobody was using it.

Here's what went wrong — and honestly, it took me too long to admit this:

We measured what was easy to measure. Model performance. Latency. Error rates. All the things that make engineers feel good and executives nod along in quarterly reviews.

What we didn't measure: Did it change a single decision? Did anyone's Monday look different because of it? Did it touch a workflow that actually mattered to the business?

It didn't. And we knew it, quietly, somewhere in the back of our heads.

The shift I made after:

Before writing a single line of code, I now ask three questions.
1. Who is the specific person this changes something for?
2. What do they do *differently* on Tuesday because this exists?
3. How will we know in 30 days, not 6 months?

That's it. Embarrassingly simple. But it forced us to tie every AI initiative to a behavior change, not a benchmark.

Perfect metrics and zero impact isn't a technical failure. It's a strategy failure dressed up in a lab coat.

The hardest part? Most AI pilots I see are still making the same mistake. Beautiful models solving for problems nobody owns.

If you're running an AI pilot right now — what's the one human behavior it's supposed to change? Can you name it?

If the answer takes more than one sentence, that might be your real problem.
