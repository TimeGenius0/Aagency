Most CEOs I talk to still think AI risk lives in the IT department.

Firewall it. Audit it. Put a policy doc in a drawer somewhere. Done.

But the CEOs who are actually winning with AI right now? They've made a different mental shift. They treat model behavior the same way they treat product behavior.

Meaning: if your AI says something wrong, hallucinates a fact, or gives a customer bad advice — that's not a tech failure. That's a product failure. It has your brand on it. It ships to real users. It carries real consequences.

The distinction sounds subtle. It's not.

When AI risk is an IT problem, you get compliance checklists.
When AI risk is a product problem, you get feedback loops, red-teaming, monitoring dashboards, and someone accountable who isn't just the CISO.

I've seen companies burn months building impressive-looking AI tools that quietly eroded user trust because nobody owned the "is this thing behaving correctly?" question at the product level.

The best operators I know have started asking their teams: *What does this model do under pressure? At the edges? When the user gives it weird input?*

That's not an IT question. That's a product quality question.

And it requires the same rigor you'd give any customer-facing feature — not a one-time security review.

AI is shipping as product now. Leadership needs to govern it like product.

So — does your organization treat model behavior as a product risk or an IT risk? I'm genuinely curious where most teams are sitting on this.
