Most companies are sleepwalking into a talent crisis they don't even see coming.

AI isn't going to replace your knowledge workers evenly. It's going to split them into two groups — fast.

Group one: the people who learn to use AI as a force multiplier. Their output doubles. Their judgment gets sharper because they're spending less time on low-signal work. They become almost unfairly productive.

Group two: the people whose entire value was trapped in tasks AI now does better, cheaper, and faster. Not because they're bad at their jobs. Because nobody helped them evolve.

The uncomfortable truth? Most talent strategies aren't making this choice deliberately. They're just hoping it works out.

I've been in rooms with senior leaders who talk about AI adoption in terms of tools and licenses. Almost none of them are talking about which roles are being fundamentally restructured — and which people in those roles have the adaptability to make the jump.

That's not an IT decision. That's a workforce strategy decision. And it's overdue.

The highest-leverage move right now isn't buying another AI platform. It's figuring out which of your highest-paid people are on a path to becoming 10x more valuable — and actually investing in that path. And being honest about who isn't.

This isn't ruthless. Done well, it's actually the more humane approach. Because the worst outcome is waiting until the gap is obvious and the options are gone.

Where is your organization on this? Are you actively designing for both outcomes — or just watching to see what happens?
