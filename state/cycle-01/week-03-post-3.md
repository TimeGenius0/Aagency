Nobody warned us it would feel this uncomfortable.

Last month I finally got our exec team in a room with AI tools running — not a vendor demo, not a consultant presenting slides. Actual working session. Prompts going in, outputs coming out, us trying to figure out what to do with them.

Here's what actually happened:

Our CFO typed her first prompt like she was filling out a form. Very formal. Very structured. Got mediocre output and concluded AI was overrated.

Our COO immediately tried to use it for something wildly complex with zero context and then blamed the tool when it hallucinated.

One VP quietly got genuinely useful output, said nothing, and closed the laptop.

And me? I over-explained everything to the model like I was training a nervous intern.

The awkward truth nobody tells you about AI adoption at the leadership level: **the first real session exposes your thinking habits, not the technology's limitations.**

Bad prompts aren't a prompt problem. They're a clarity-of-thought problem. If you can't explain what you actually need to another human in plain language, you're not going to get it from an AI either.

What changed after that session wasn't our tool stack. It was our willingness to admit we were all beginners at something we'd been nodding along to for two years.

That's the real unlock.

If your executive team hasn't sat together and actually *used* AI tools — not watched someone use them — you're building strategy on assumptions.

What did your team's first real AI working session look like? Did it surface the same awkwardness?
