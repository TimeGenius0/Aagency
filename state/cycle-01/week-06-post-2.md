Your competitor just announced an "AI-first transformation initiative."

Your board forwarded the press release with a single line: *"Are we doing this?"*

And now you're in a room trying to reverse-engineer someone else's strategy instead of building your own.

This is how companies get hurt right now.

Here's what that press release didn't tell you:
— They don't know if it's working yet
— The announcement *is* the strategy (which is a problem)
— They're watching YOU for the same signal

Benchmarking against peers in a period of genuine discontinuity is uniquely dangerous. In stable markets, it's lazy. Right now, it's actively misleading.

Your competitor's AI roadmap was built on their constraints, their talent gaps, their board dynamics, their customer mix. It has almost nothing to do with yours.

The companies I'm watching who are actually getting this right share one thing: they started from their own operational pain, not from an industry announcement. They picked two or three high-friction workflows, ran real experiments, measured real outcomes, and built from there.

Unglamorous. No press release required.

The ones struggling? They're in PowerPoint trying to match a peer's ambition without understanding the underlying logic.

I'm not saying ignore your competitive landscape. I'm saying your strategy signal should come from your customers and your operations — not from someone else's comms team.

The hardest discipline in this moment is resisting the pressure to react and instead forcing yourself to originate.

So — what's actually driving your AI priorities right now? Internal pain points, or external pressure?

Be honest. The answer matters more than most leaders want to admit.
