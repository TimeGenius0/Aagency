Most AI experiments end the same way: a deck with "key learnings" and no decision made.

That's not experimentation. That's expensive journaling.

If you're running AI pilots without a structured cadence, you're probably generating insights that evaporate in the next all-hands and never actually change how you operate.

Here's what a decision-producing cadence looks like in practice:

**1. Start with the decision, not the experiment**
Before you run anything, write down: "At the end of this, we will decide whether to ___." If you can't fill in that blank, you're not ready to start.

**2. Set a forcing function date**
Not a "readout." A decision meeting. There's a difference. One produces slides. The other produces accountability.

**3. Define your kill criteria upfront**
What would make you stop? Most teams only define success metrics. Defining failure metrics is what separates disciplined experimentation from sunk cost theater.

**4. Assign a decision owner, not a project owner**
Someone needs to be empowered to say yes or no — not just summarize findings. These are different people with different incentives.

**5. Timebox ruthlessly**
Two weeks max for early-stage AI pilots. The models are improving faster than your 90-day experiment can keep up with anyway.

The uncomfortable truth: most organizations aren't bad at AI. They're bad at making decisions under uncertainty. The experimentation cadence is just where that weakness becomes visible.

Fix the decision infrastructure first. The AI stuff gets easier after that.
