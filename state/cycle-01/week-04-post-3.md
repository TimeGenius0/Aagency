Most AI literacy programs are just expensive checkbox exercises.

Here's what I've watched happen dozens of times: company announces AI training initiative, employees sit through 4-hour workshops, everyone gets a certificate, leadership feels good, nothing changes.

Six months later? Same workflows. Same habits. Same skepticism.

The problem isn't the content. The problem is that behavior change doesn't happen in classrooms — it happens in consequence structures.

What actually moves the needle:

**Visible executive modeling.** When the CEO shares their actual AI workflow in an all-hands — not a polished case study, a messy real one — adoption accelerates faster than any training ever could. People copy status, not curriculum.

**Embedded accountability.** Teams that build AI tool usage into their weekly standups, retrospectives, sprint reviews — not as surveillance but as shared practice — develop habits that stick. The ritual matters more than the rationale.

**Small wins, fast.** The single best behavior change driver I've seen is getting someone to save 45 minutes on something they hate doing. That one moment does more than 8 hours of "AI strategy" content.

**Permission to experiment badly.** Most employees won't try new tools if failure feels risky. Psychological safety isn't soft — it's the actual precondition for adoption at scale.

Literacy programs teach people *about* AI. None of that matters until someone uses it, fails, adjusts, and tries again.

The organizations winning on AI adoption aren't running better training. They're building better feedback loops.

If you're responsible for AI adoption at your company — what's the one structural change that actually shifted behavior? I'm collecting patterns.
