Buying an enterprise AI platform doesn't make you an AI company. It makes you a company with an expensive subscription.

I've watched this movie before. It was called "digital transformation." Organizations bought the tools, announced the initiative, hired a Chief Digital Officer, and then... mostly nothing changed. The tools sat underutilized. The org chart shifted. The strategy deck got updated.

We're doing it again.

The tells are everywhere:
— Procurement moves faster than use case discovery
— The vendor demo becomes the internal vision
— Success gets measured by licenses deployed, not problems solved
— Nobody can name the three workflows that actually changed

Here's the uncomfortable truth: platforms don't create strategy. They reveal whether you have one.

If you don't know which decisions you want AI to accelerate, which data you trust enough to act on, or which human judgment you're *not* willing to automate — a platform just gives you a more expensive way to be confused.

The companies quietly winning right now aren't the ones with the biggest AI contracts. They're the ones who started with a problem worth solving, ran a scrappy experiment, learned something real, and built from there.

That's boring. It doesn't make for a great press release. But it compounds.

The platform can come later. The thinking has to come first.

So honest question — if someone asked you today to name the three highest-value decisions your AI investment is actually improving, could you answer in under 60 seconds?

If not, that's where the strategy work starts.
