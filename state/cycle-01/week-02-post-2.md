11 AI pilots. One survivor.

That's the outcome one $2B industrial manufacturer landed on after 18 months of experimentation. And honestly? The brutal part wasn't killing the pilots. It was admitting they never had real selection criteria to begin with.

Here's what actually ended up on their evaluation scorecard — after the fact:

**1. Does it touch a workflow that runs every single day?**
Weekly or monthly processes got deprioritized immediately. Frequency creates compounding ROI.

**2. Can a non-technical operator explain what the AI is doing?**
If the end user couldn't articulate it, adoption died quietly. Every time.

**3. Does failure have a recoverable cost?**
The pilots that died fastest were the ones where a bad output was either invisible or catastrophic. Neither extreme works.

**4. Is there a human whose job gets measurably easier — not eliminated?**
The one that survived was championed by a 22-year plant floor veteran who started calling it "my assistant." That signal mattered more than any projected ROI number.

The winning use case? Maintenance log summarization feeding into parts ordering decisions. Unglamorous. Invisible from the outside. Running 6 days a week.

The lesson I keep coming back to: most companies design AI pilots around what's technically impressive. The ones that stick were designed around what's operationally unavoidable.

Killing 11 things is only smart if you know *why* you're keeping the one.

What criteria is your organization actually using to evaluate which AI initiatives survive? I'm genuinely curious whether anyone has formalized this or if it's still mostly gut feel.
