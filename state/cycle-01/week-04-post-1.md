Everyone's hiring AI engineers right now.

And they should. But that's not actually the bottleneck.

The real bottleneck is the person who can sit in a room with a CFO, understand what's actually keeping her up at night, then walk out and translate that into something a model can work with — and then translate the output *back* into something she'll actually trust and act on.

That person is rare. Almost embarrassingly rare.

I've watched companies deploy genuinely impressive AI infrastructure that goes almost completely unused. Not because the tech failed. Because nobody built the bridge between "here's what this can do" and "here's why you should care."

AI engineers can build the engine. What organizations are starving for is someone who understands the terrain.

This role doesn't have a clean title yet. I've seen it called AI product manager, AI strategist, transformation lead. Doesn't matter what you call it. What matters is the skill stack:

- Deep enough technical literacy to smell nonsense
- Deep enough business fluency to know what actually moves the needle
- Enough interpersonal range to get a skeptical VP to try something new

Most companies are trying to hire their way out of this with more engineers. A few are quietly investing in translators — and those are the ones actually moving.

If you're leading AI transformation right now, ask yourself honestly: do you have people who can do both? Or do you have two separate groups talking past each other while the roadmap stalls?

This is the hire that unlocks everything else.

Who's playing this role in your org — and did you find them or build them?
