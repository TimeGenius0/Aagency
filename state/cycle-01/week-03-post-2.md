Most AI business cases die in the CFO's office. Not because the idea is bad. Because the math is embarrassing.

Here's what I see constantly: Someone builds an 18-month roadmap, loads it with "productivity gains" and "efficiency improvements," slaps a 3x ROI on the cover page, and then can't answer a single follow-up question without saying "we'll need to track that post-launch."

That's not a business case. That's a wish list with a spreadsheet attached.

If you want your AI investment to survive the first 10 minutes, structure it around three questions a CFO actually asks:

**1. What stops working if we don't do this?**
Status quo cost is more compelling than upside potential. Make the cost of inaction concrete.

**2. What's the minimum viable proof point — and how fast can we get there?**
CFOs don't hate AI. They hate infinite runways. Show a 60-90 day checkpoint with a killswitch.

**3. Who owns the number?**
Not the team. One person. With a name. If ownership is diffuse, credibility is zero.

The companies I've watched actually get AI budgets approved aren't pitching transformation. They're pitching a contained experiment with a measurable outcome and an honest answer to "what could go wrong."

That's it. That's the whole game.

The vision can live in your strategy deck. The business case needs to live in reality.
