Handing AI strategy to your CTO is like handing your growth strategy to your CFO.

Technically competent. Fundamentally misaligned.

I get why it happens. AI feels like a technology problem. CTOs speak the language. It's a clean org chart decision that lets the CEO stay focused on "the business."

But here's what I keep seeing: companies where the CTO owns AI end up with great infrastructure and mediocre transformation. The models are deployed. The APIs are connected. And almost nothing about how the company actually *operates* has changed.

AI strategy isn't a technology decision. It's a business model decision. It's a talent decision. It's a "what do we actually believe humans should be doing here" decision.

That last one especially. No CTO should be answering that alone.

The CEOs who are getting this right aren't becoming AI experts. They're staying deeply curious and opinionated about *where* AI changes the unit economics of their business — and then building around that thesis. They're in the room when the hard calls get made about which workflows get automated and which ones don't.

When the CEO delegates AI strategy entirely, what they're really delegating is their point of view on the future of the company. That's not a technology handoff. That's an abdication.

The CTO should absolutely own implementation. But the CEO needs to own the *vision* of what AI makes possible — and what it demands you become.

I'm curious — for those of you inside organizations navigating this: who actually owns your AI strategy? And does it match where the real decisions are getting made?
