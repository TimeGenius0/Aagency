Our old strategic planning template was basically useless the moment we started taking AI seriously.

Not because it was badly designed. It was actually pretty solid. But it was built for a world where the future was *uncertain in predictable ways* — market shifts, competitive moves, regulatory changes. Things you could model with reasonable confidence intervals.

AI broke that assumption completely.

So we rewrote the whole thing. Here's what changed:

**Old template asked:** What are our 3-year goals?
**New template asks:** Which of our 3-year goals survive across *all* plausible AI scenarios?

**Old template had:** SWOT analysis
**New template has:** A "capability half-life" audit — which internal skills are durable vs. which ones AI commoditizes within 18 months?

**Old template tracked:** KPIs
**New template tracks:** KPIs *plus* leading indicators that one of our scenarios is becoming the dominant one

The biggest shift was forcing ourselves to plan in parallel tracks instead of a single roadmap. We now maintain three live scenario paths — conservative AI adoption, moderate, and accelerated — and we review which track we're actually on every quarter.

It sounds like more work. It is more work. But the old way was just false precision. We were writing confident plans built on assumptions we hadn't examined in two years.

The hardest part wasn't building the new template. It was convincing the leadership team that "we're not sure yet" is a legitimate strategic answer — as long as you have a clear tripwire for when you'll decide.
