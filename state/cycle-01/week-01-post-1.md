Your AI steering committee is slowing you down more than any technology gap ever could.

I know that's not what you want to hear — especially if you spent six months getting the right stakeholders in the room.

But here's what I keep seeing: companies stand up governance structures designed to manage risk, then wonder why they can't move. You've got legal, compliance, IT security, and three business unit leads all required to sign off before anyone can run a pilot. The result isn't safety. It's paralysis dressed up as due diligence.

The painful irony is that the companies treating AI like a regulated pharmaceutical are losing ground to competitors who just... started. They iterated. They broke small things. They learned.

Governance has its place. I'm not arguing for reckless deployment of AI in high-stakes environments. But most organizations are applying nuclear-grade oversight to use cases that carry roughly the same risk as adopting a new Excel plugin.

The question worth asking your committee: are we governing against actual risk, or are we governing against the *feeling* of risk?

Because those are very different things — and they produce very different outcomes.

The fastest AI adopters I've seen aren't ungoverned. They're *selectively* governed. They know which decisions need a committee and which ones need a 48-hour sandbox test.

Speed is a strategic asset right now. And bureaucracy has a compounding cost most leaders aren't measuring.

What would change in your organization if pilots required approval in days, not quarters?
