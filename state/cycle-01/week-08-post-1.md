Most responsible AI frameworks are useless to the people actually deploying AI.

I've read the McKinsey ones. The Deloitte ones. The Accenture ones. They're beautifully formatted. They cite the EU AI Act. They have governance matrices and stakeholder maps.

And they are written for the person presenting to the board — not the person deciding whether to let an AI agent touch customer data on a Tuesday afternoon.

Here's what a framework that actually helps operators looks like:

**1. Decision rights, not principles**
Who can approve what class of AI use case? Name the person. Not "the governance committee."

**2. Failure mode taxonomy**
What are your three most likely ways this goes wrong? Hallucination? Data leakage? Model drift? Pick the real ones for your context.

**3. A 48-hour rule**
If something breaks, can you explain what happened, to whom, and how you fixed it — within 48 hours? If not, you're not ready to deploy.

**4. Operator-level checklists, not executive summaries**
The person running the prompt, not the person signing the strategy doc, needs the guardrails.

**5. Living documents with owners**
A framework with no named owner and no review date is a liability document pretending to be a governance document.

The big consultancies write for regulators because that's who pays for the engagement. That's not a criticism — it's just a business model reality.

But if you're actually trying to run AI responsibly inside a real organization, you need something that fits in a Notion doc and gets updated when something breaks.
