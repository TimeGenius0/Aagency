I asked our top 10 leaders how they used AI last week.

Not in a survey. In a room. Face to face.

The answers were... revealing.

3 of them had genuinely woven it into their daily work. Drafting, synthesizing, pressure-testing their own thinking. They spoke about it the way you talk about a tool you actually trust.

4 of them had tried it a few times. Mostly for writing help. Mostly disappointed. Mostly moved on.

3 of them hadn't really used it at all.

And here's the thing — these are smart, capable, high-performing leaders. This isn't a talent problem.

It's a habit problem. A belief problem. Maybe a psychological safety problem.

When I dug deeper, the pattern that emerged was this: the leaders who were using AI had given themselves *permission to be bad at it first.* They'd experimented without needing it to be impressive. They'd failed quietly and kept going.

The others were still waiting for the moment it felt ready. Or safe. Or worth their time.

We're in week 5 of an 8-week transformation sprint, and I thought by now we'd be further along behaviorally. The tools are deployed. The training happened. The strategy deck is airtight.

But strategy doesn't change behavior. Curiosity does. Psychological safety does. Leaders who model the fumbling do.

So that's my next focus — not more enablement. More modeling. More public learning. More leaders willing to say "here's what I tried and here's what was awkward about it."

Transformation lives in the habits of the people at the top.

What changed YOUR relationship with AI — a training, a tool, or watching someone you respect actually use it?
