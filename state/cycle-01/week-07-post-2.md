Most AI pilots fail not because the technology is bad — but because teams picked the wrong workflow to start with.

Here's the framework I use to find the three workflows worth betting on first:

**1. High frequency × high friction**
If someone does it 10+ times a week and it's annoying every single time, AI compounds fast here. The ROI math is brutal and obvious. Start here if you need to build internal credibility quickly.

**2. Bottleneck to something downstream**
Find where work piles up waiting for a human decision or output. AI doesn't just speed up that step — it unlocks everything behind it. One unblock can feel like hiring three people.

**3. Consistency matters more than creativity**
Workflows where variance is the enemy (compliance language, support responses, reporting) are underrated AI targets. The goal isn't brilliance — it's reliable, good-enough output at scale.

The mistake I see constantly: teams chase the *impressive* use case instead of the *compounding* one. A demo-worthy AI feature that runs twice a month is worse than a boring one that saves 20 minutes every single day.

When you're resource-constrained, sequencing is everything. You don't need 10 AI projects. You need three that build momentum and fund the next wave.

Map your workflows against those three filters. Stack-rank them. Start with whatever scores highest across all three.

It's not sexy. But it's how you actually build internal proof that earns more budget, more trust, and more room to experiment.
