Most CEOs restructure around AI and just rename the org chart.

One CEO I know actually blew it up.

Here's what happened when she got serious about AI-augmented decision-making:

**Roles that quietly disappeared:**
- Chief of Staff (replaced by an AI system that synthesizes board prep, competitive intel, and weekly priorities)
- Two VP-level "information aggregator" roles — people whose primary value was knowing things and summarizing them for leadership
- A market research function that was basically a $400K/year news curation service

None of these people were fired dramatically. Two moved into new roles. One left. The function just... dissolved because the work dissolved.

**Roles that emerged:**
- A "Decision Architecture" lead — someone who designs *how* the leadership team takes in information and makes calls, not just what they decide
- An AI Systems Integrator on the exec team (not in IT, *on the exec team*)
- A dedicated "Assumption Auditor" — someone whose job is to pressure-test the confident conclusions the AI keeps surfacing

The hardest part wasn't the restructuring. It was getting her leadership team to admit that a lot of their identity was wrapped up in being the person who *knew stuff*.

When AI can surface the knowing, the value shifts to judgment, synthesis, and the courage to act on incomplete information.

That's a different skill set. And not everyone makes the transition.

The question I keep asking leaders: **Are you restructuring your team around AI's capabilities, or just buying AI tools for the team you already have?**

Because those lead to very different outcomes 18 months from now.
